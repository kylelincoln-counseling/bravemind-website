VRET Protocol Guidelines

Rationale for Virtual Reality Exposure

The following script can be used to explain the rationale for VRET to the patient.

“As we have discussed previously, exposure is the first line of treatment for posttraumatic stress disorder because of its proven effectiveness. People take their experiences throughout life and file them away in their minds. When a trauma comes along, it is so immense and unfathomable we do not know how to file it... The exposure exercise is designed to force us to truly sort through the experience. By repeating the experience over and over it allows our mind to make some sort of sense of it and file it away. Once it is filed, it is no longer as intrusive. This technique may seem counterintuitive, as our natural reaction is not to dwell on disturbing memories; however, it has been shown to work.”

“For this treatment, we will be using the virtual reality to aid in the exposure exercise. The multiplicity of sensory cues that VR affords provides a greater possibility of generating a sense of presence that can facilitate processing of the traumatic experience... As you view the scenes through the helmet, I will be watching the same images simultaneously on the computer screen. We will be progressing through a variety of scenes that approximate what happened in your experience.”

In-Session Instructions for the Patient

Once the patient is in the virtual environment, the following instructions can be given:

“I will begin now by showing you the first scene. As you view the scene I'd like you to just describe whatever comes to your mind in as much detail as possible. It may be a specific memory, a thought, smell, sight, feeling or your entire experience. Just try to re-experience whatever it is that you are describing, and to that end remember to speak in the first person and the present tense. I will ask you for your SUDS rating every 5 minutes. Please tell me your SUDS rating now before beginning.”

Working with Hot Spots

When the patient is ready, introduce 'hot spots.' Hot spots are the specific moments in the trauma memory that cause more anxiety than the rest of the narrative. It is often the moment an explosion occurred, gunfire started, or when the patient or a comrade was injured or thought they would die. The therapist should make notes during exposure about which moments seem to be the hardest for the patient. We recommend starting with the worst and repeatedly exposing the patient to that in minute detail, over and over, until it gets easier. At that point, move on to the next highest hot spot and repeat as above.

Breathing Retraining

Breathing retraining is a foundational skill taught early in treatment to help manage anxiety.

  1. Take a normal breath in through your nose with your mouth closed.
  2. Exhale slowly with your mouth closed.
  3. On exhaling, silently say to yourself the word CALM or RELAX very slowly.
  4. Pause, holding your breath, for a count of 4 and then take the next inhalation.
