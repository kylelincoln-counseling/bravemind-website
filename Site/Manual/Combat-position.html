Main Menu

The main menu of the BRAVEMIND Ukraine application allows the clinician to select the world, language, and whether visual elements such as blood will be present. In the VR2 walkthrough, clinicians begin by selecting the Combat Position world, confirming default settings, and launching the scenario. As shown in Figure 1, the interface is structured to highlight current selections and minimize setup confusion for first-time users.

Combat Position Control Interface

The Control Interface is the clinician's dashboard for managing the VR scenario in real time. From this screen, the operator can activate triggers, adjust settings, and change the participant’s location. As shown in Figure 2, the interface displays all available triggers in a clear grid layout. Triggers include auditory cues (like rifle fire), visual-auditory combinations (like drone attacks), and full scenario transitions (like the prisoner of war scene). Some triggers open a secondary control panel as a pop-up display.

Figure 3 shows the Explosion Nearby trigger, which can be adjusted to simulate an impact between 10–30 meters from the participant and from a specified direction.

Figure 4 displays additional interface elements that support trigger customization and confirm when a trigger is active. These tools allow the clinician to adapt the scenario responsively while maintaining immersion. Figures 5 allows the operating clinician to minimize the Control Interface.

Points of Relocation

The environment allows clinicians to move the participant to five preset locations, each representing a different part of the combat zone. These include trench-adjacent areas, open fields, or more enclosed environments. Figures 6 through 10 display each of the relocation points and how they appear within the interface.

Seasons

Clinicians can adjust the season to match the participant’s lived experiences or to shape the emotional tone of the session. Figures 11 through 14 show the seasonal adjustments: winter, spring, summer, and autumn. Seasonal variation affects the color, lighting, and overall feel of the VR environment.

Time of Day

The simulation supports changes to time of day, allowing clinicians to set the experience to morning, noon, evening, or night. These changes alter shadows, ambient light, and mood. Figures 15 through 18 demonstrate each time-of-day setting.

Weather Conditions

Weather conditions can be layered onto the environment for further customization. Clear weather is the default, but clinicians may switch to cloudy, dust storm, fog, overcast, rain, snow, or thunderstorm depending on the clinical goals. As seen in Figures 19 through 26, these weather options are accessible through a dropdown menu and immediately alter the participant’s environment upon activation.

Triggers

Trigger Types

Triggers are grouped into three categories: 1. auditory, 2. combined visual and auditory, and 3. scenario triggers with transport and dialogue. This classification is reflected in Figures 27, 28, and 29.

1. Audio-Only Triggers

Auditory Triggers are sound-based and do not include any visual component. They include gunfire, explosions, and calls to battle, and are primarily used to simulate combat soundscapes (see Figure 27).

2. Audio and Visual Triggers

Combined Visual and Auditory Triggers include drones, helicopters, mine explosions, and panic responses. These provide both visual and auditory immersion and are designed to evoke a more intense, realistic reaction (see Figure 28).

3. Scenario Triggers

Scenario Triggers with Dialogue and Transport relocate the participant to a new virtual scene and present scripted audio. These are shown in Figure 29.
